CITP FAQ Page

What is the purpose of the NIA/NIH's Caenorhabditis Intervention Testing Program (CITP)?

The CITP tests compounds for their ability to extend lifespan and/or healthspans in Caenorhabditis worms.

Why Caenorhabditis?

Caenorhabditis species are excellent model organisms for the purpose of this study due to their short lifespans and genetic tractability. They are simple to prepare for experimentation because the worms self-fertilize.

How are lifespan experiments conducted?

Hermaphroditic Caenorhabditis strains are placed on agar plates treated with the compound of interest. These species of Caenorhabditis include C. elegans, C. briggsae, and C. tropicalis. Each worm is observed until death, whether from natural causes or other means (bagging, extrusion, etc.).

Where are the experiments conducted?

The experiments are conducted in research laboratories at three different institutions: the Buck Institute for Research on Aging, Rutgers University, and the University of Oregon.

If the agent shortens the lifespan or has negative effects, what will happen to the data?

All of the CITP data is published, including data on agents that fail to increase lifespan and/or healthspan, or interventions that have deleterious effects.

What is CeleST data?

C. elegans swim test software (CeleST) tracks the animals' swimming behaviors to report on dynamics of posture and speed. The tracking provides coordinates of the central body line to enter movement data. These machines generate data on swimming locomotion to be analyzed to evaluate the behaviors elicited.
